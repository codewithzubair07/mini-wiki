# Source: Zettelkasten: The Note-Taking Method Behind Compounding Knowledge

## Summary
A note on the Zettelkasten method invented by sociologist Niklas Luhmann — a slip-box system of atomic, interlinked index cards that allowed him to produce an extraordinary body of work. The note traces Zettelkasten's connection to Vannevar Bush's Memex, its digital successors (Obsidian, Roam), and explains why its core principles (atomic notes, explicit links, emergent structure) directly underpin the LLM Wiki architecture.

## Key Claims
- Niklas Luhmann's physical Zettelkasten contained ~90,000 cards and supported 70+ books and 400+ articles
- The core insight: knowledge compounds when notes are linked rather than filed in isolation
- Four principles: atomic notes, permanent notes written in own words, explicit links to existing notes, no rigid hierarchy
- Luhmann's Zettelkasten is the closest pre-digital realisation of Vannevar Bush's Memex "associative trails" idea
- Modern tools (Obsidian, Roam Research, Logseq) are direct digital successors of Zettelkasten
- The LLM Wiki pattern extends Zettelkasten further: the LLM acts as an automated note-taker maintaining the slip box
- The "one wiki page per entity/concept" rule maps to atomic notes; wikilinks implement Zettelkasten linking discipline
- `index.md` plays the role of Luhmann's register (keyword index) — entry points into the knowledge network
